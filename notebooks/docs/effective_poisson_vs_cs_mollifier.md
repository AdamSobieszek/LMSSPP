# Effective Euclidean Poisson Potential vs the Standard CS Mollifier

This note rewrites the Euclideanized Möbius-Poisson family in the form that is most directly comparable to the mollified Cucker-Smale kernel used in the weak-solution literature.

The exact radial quantile transport from the Euclideanized Poisson family to the weakly singular CS Gibbs family is not repeated here. That belongs in the appendix. The purpose of this note is narrower:

- identify the effective Euclidean pair potential suggested by the Möbius-Poisson picture,
- compare its gradient and Hessian directly to the standard mollified kernel
  $$
  W_\varepsilon(x)
  =
  \frac{1}{2-\alpha}(|x|+\varepsilon)^{1-\alpha}
  \left(|x|-\frac{\varepsilon}{1-\alpha}\right),
  \qquad \alpha\in(0,1),
  $$
- extract the local regime where the two are comparable,
- make precise the global obstruction to identifying them.

As in the previous note, I use:

- $n$ for the Euclidean ambient dimension,
- $n=d$ when comparing to the CS system in $\mathbb R^d$.

## 1. Effective Euclidean Pair Kernel

From the stereographic / Cayley image of the Möbius-Poisson family one obtains the Euclidean density
$$
p_{n,s}(z)\propto (s^2+|z|^2)^{-n}.
$$
Up to an irrelevant additive constant in the potential, the corresponding effective Euclidean radial potential is
$$
\Phi_{n,s}(z):=n\log(s^2+|z|^2),\qquad z\in \mathbb R^n.
$$

For comparison with the CS convolution kernel, it is best to regard $\Phi_{n,s}$ as a translation-invariant pair interaction
$$
\Phi_{n,s}(x-x').
$$

The first-order velocity field generated by this potential is then
$$
u^{\mathrm{PK}}_{n,s}[\rho](x,\omega)
:=
\omega-(\nabla \Phi_{n,s}*\rho)(x).
$$

The standard mollified CS field from the theorem fragment is
$$
u_\varepsilon[\rho](x,\omega)
:=
\omega-(\nabla W_\varepsilon*\rho)(x).
$$

So the comparison reduces to $\nabla \Phi_{n,s}$ versus $\nabla W_\varepsilon$, and more structurally to $D^2\Phi_{n,s}$ versus $D^2W_\varepsilon$.

## 2. Radial Formulas

Write
$$
r=|z|,\qquad \hat z=\frac{z}{|z|}\quad (z\neq 0).
$$

Then
$$
\nabla \Phi_{n,s}(z)
=
\frac{2n}{s^2+r^2}\,z.
$$

The Hessian admits the radial decomposition
$$
D^2\Phi_{n,s}(z)
=
\lambda_{\perp}^{\mathrm{PK}}(r)\,(I_n-\hat z\otimes\hat z)
+\lambda_{\parallel}^{\mathrm{PK}}(r)\,\hat z\otimes\hat z,
\qquad z\neq 0,
$$
with
$$
\lambda_{\perp}^{\mathrm{PK}}(r)=\frac{2n}{s^2+r^2},
\qquad
\lambda_{\parallel}^{\mathrm{PK}}(r)=\frac{2n(s^2-r^2)}{(s^2+r^2)^2}.
$$
At the origin this extends continuously as
$$
D^2\Phi_{n,s}(0)=\frac{2n}{s^2}I_n.
$$

For the standard mollifier,
$$
\nabla W_\varepsilon(z)
=
\frac{1}{1-\alpha}(r+\varepsilon)^{-\alpha}z,
$$
and
$$
D^2W_\varepsilon(z)
=
\lambda_{\perp}^{\varepsilon}(r)\,(I_n-\hat z\otimes\hat z)
+\lambda_{\parallel}^{\varepsilon}(r)\,\hat z\otimes\hat z,
\qquad z\neq 0,
$$
with
$$
\lambda_{\perp}^{\varepsilon}(r)
=
\frac{1}{1-\alpha}(r+\varepsilon)^{-\alpha},
$$
$$
\lambda_{\parallel}^{\varepsilon}(r)
=
\frac{(1-\alpha)r+\varepsilon}{(1-\alpha)(r+\varepsilon)^{\alpha+1}}.
$$
Again,
$$
D^2W_\varepsilon(0)=\frac{1}{1-\alpha}\varepsilon^{-\alpha}I_n.
$$

## 3. Main Comparison

**Proposition 1 (Direct comparison of the effective Poisson kernel and the CS mollifier).**
Let $n\ge 1$, $s>0$, $\alpha\in(0,1)$, and $\varepsilon>0$. Then the following statements hold.

1. **Core stiffness matching.**
   There is a unique choice of mollification scale
   $$
   \varepsilon_\ast=\varepsilon_\ast(n,s,\alpha)
   :=
   \left(\frac{s^2}{2n(1-\alpha)}\right)^{1/\alpha}
   $$
   such that
   $$
   D^2\Phi_{n,s}(0)=D^2W_{\varepsilon_\ast}(0).
   $$

2. **Local quadratic agreement.**
   As $r\to 0$,
   $$
   \nabla \Phi_{n,s}(z)
   =
   \left(\frac{2n}{s^2}+O\!\left(\frac{r^2}{s^4}\right)\right)z,
   $$
   while
   $$
   \nabla W_{\varepsilon_\ast}(z)
   =
   \left(\frac{2n}{s^2}+O\!\left(\frac{n}{s^2}\frac{r}{\varepsilon_\ast}\right)\right)z.
   $$
   Hence, after matching the center stiffness, the two kernels agree at leading order in the small-displacement regime.

3. **Global convexity mismatch.**
   For every $r\ge 0$,
   $$
   \lambda_{\perp}^{\varepsilon}(r)>0,
   \qquad
   \lambda_{\parallel}^{\varepsilon}(r)>0.
   $$
   So $W_\varepsilon$ is globally convex.

   By contrast,
   $$
   \lambda_{\parallel}^{\mathrm{PK}}(r)
   \begin{cases}
   >0, & 0\le r<s,\\
   =0, & r=s,\\
   <0, & r>s.
   \end{cases}
   $$
   Therefore $\Phi_{n,s}$ loses radial convexity exactly at $r=s$.

4. **Tail-class mismatch.**
   As $r\to\infty$,
   $$
   \lambda_{\perp}^{\mathrm{PK}}(r)\sim 2n\,r^{-2},
   \qquad
   \lambda_{\parallel}^{\mathrm{PK}}(r)\sim -2n\,r^{-2},
   $$
   whereas
   $$
   \lambda_{\perp}^{\varepsilon}(r)\sim \frac{1}{1-\alpha}r^{-\alpha},
   \qquad
   \lambda_{\parallel}^{\varepsilon}(r)\sim r^{-\alpha}.
   $$
   Since $\alpha\in(0,1)$, the decay exponents are different, and the radial eigenvalue has opposite sign in the two families.

In particular, no fixed choice of $\varepsilon>0$ can make $D^2\Phi_{n,s}$ globally equivalent to $D^2W_\varepsilon$ when $0<\alpha<1$.

### Proof Sketch

The formula for $\varepsilon_\ast$ comes from matching the isotropic Hessians at the origin:
$$
\frac{2n}{s^2}=\frac{1}{1-\alpha}\varepsilon^{-\alpha}.
$$

The local expansions follow by Taylor expansion of
$$
\frac{2n}{s^2+r^2}
$$
and
$$
(r+\varepsilon)^{-\alpha}.
$$

The convexity statement for $W_\varepsilon$ is immediate from the explicit eigenvalues, since both are positive for all $r\ge 0$.

For the Poisson kernel, the tangential eigenvalue is always positive, but the radial eigenvalue
$$
\lambda_{\parallel}^{\mathrm{PK}}(r)=\frac{2n(s^2-r^2)}{(s^2+r^2)^2}
$$
changes sign at $r=s$.

The tail asymptotics are obtained directly from the explicit formulas.

## 4. Interpretation

Proposition 1 shows that the Euclideanized Poisson kernel and the standard CS mollifier are related only in a **core-matched local sense**.

More precisely:

- the Poisson-derived potential $\Phi_{n,s}$ is a smooth core regularization with local curvature $2n/s^2$,
- the CS mollifier $W_\varepsilon$ is also smooth at the origin, and can be tuned to have the same curvature,
- but globally the two belong to different classes of radial kernels.

The distinction is qualitative, not cosmetic:

- $W_\varepsilon$ is globally convex and preserves the weakly singular tail class $r^{-\alpha}$,
- $\Phi_{n,s}$ has logarithmic growth at infinity,
  $$
  \Phi_{n,s}(z)=2n\log|z|+O(1),
  $$
  and its radial Hessian changes sign beyond the scale $r=s$.

So the Euclideanized Poisson kernel should not be interpreted as a direct replacement for the standard CS mollifier. It is a different effective regularization, with a different long-range geometry.

## 5. High-Dimensional Consequences

The previous note showed that the probability density
$$
p_{n,s}(z)\propto e^{-\Phi_{n,s}(z)}=(s^2+|z|^2)^{-n}
$$
has the radial law
$$
\frac{|Z|^2}{s^2+|Z|^2}\sim \mathrm{Beta}\!\left(\frac n2,\frac n2\right),
$$
hence
$$
\frac{|Z|}{s}\to 1
\qquad\text{in probability as } n\to\infty.
$$

So in high dimension the associated Euclideanized Poisson family concentrates on a thin shell near $r=s$.

This shell scale is exactly where the radial eigenvalue of the effective potential changes sign:
$$
\lambda_{\parallel}^{\mathrm{PK}}(s)=0.
$$
That is a useful geometric marker:

- for $r<s$, the effective Poisson potential is radially convex,
- near $r=s$, the radial curvature degenerates,
- for $r>s$, the radial direction becomes effectively concave.

No analogous transition occurs for the standard mollified CS kernel.

## 6. Dimension Scaling and Center Matching

The center-matching mollification scale is
$$
\varepsilon_\ast(n,s,\alpha)
=
\left(\frac{s^2}{2n(1-\alpha)}\right)^{1/\alpha}.
$$

This gives two natural regimes.

**Corollary 2 (Large-$n$ scaling of the matched mollifier).**

1. If $s$ is fixed as $n\to\infty$, then
   $$
   \varepsilon_\ast(n,s,\alpha)\sim C_{\alpha,s}\,n^{-1/\alpha}\to 0.
   $$
   So matching the increasingly stiff Poisson core requires an increasingly sharp CS mollifier.

2. If instead
   $$
   s^2=\kappa n
   $$
   for some fixed $\kappa>0$, then
   $$
   D^2\Phi_{n,s}(0)=\frac{2}{\kappa}I_n
   $$
   stays $O(1)$, and
   $$
   \varepsilon_\ast(n,s,\alpha)\to
   \left(\frac{\kappa}{2(1-\alpha)}\right)^{1/\alpha}.
   $$
   This is the natural scaling if one wants a nontrivial large-dimensional local comparison at the core.

### Proof Sketch

Both claims follow immediately from the explicit formula for $\varepsilon_\ast$.

## 7. Local Gaussian Limit and Its Meaning

In the $n^{-1/2}$ core scaling
$$
z=\frac{s}{\sqrt{2n}}y,
$$
the effective Poisson potential satisfies
$$
\Phi_{n,s}(z)=\text{const}_n+\frac{|y|^2}{2}-\frac{|y|^4}{8n}+O\!\left(\frac{|y|^6}{n^2}\right).
$$

So in the very core, the Euclideanized Poisson family is asymptotically Gaussian. This again supports the local comparison with a smooth mollifier.

However, this is only a **core asymptotic**. At the measure level the mass still concentrates on the shell $|z|\approx s$, and at the kernel level the Hessian still changes sign at $r=s$. So the Gaussian core does not remove the global mismatch described in Proposition 1.

## 8. Consequence for the First-Order CS-Type Field

If one introduces the Poisson-derived first-order field
$$
u^{\mathrm{PK}}_{n,s}[\rho](x,\omega)
=
\omega-(\nabla \Phi_{n,s}*\rho)(x),
$$
then the comparison with the standard mollified field
$$
u_\varepsilon[\rho](x,\omega)
=
\omega-(\nabla W_\varepsilon*\rho)(x)
$$
is best summarized as follows.

**Conclusion.**

- In the small-displacement regime, after choosing $\varepsilon=\varepsilon_\ast(n,s,\alpha)$, the two fields have the same leading linear stiffness at the origin.
- Away from the core they diverge structurally: $u^{\mathrm{PK}}_{n,s}$ comes from a logarithmic-tail, shell-forming effective potential, while $u_\varepsilon$ comes from a globally convex mollification of the weakly singular CS kernel.
- Therefore any exact identification must pass through an additional radial remapping, not through direct equality of kernels.

That additional remapping belongs in the appendix. For the main text, the useful statement is that the Euclideanized Möbius-Poisson potential is locally comparable to the standard CS mollifier after curvature matching, but globally it defines a genuinely different radial interaction.
